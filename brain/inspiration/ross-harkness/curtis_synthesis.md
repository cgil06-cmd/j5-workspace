## Curtis's Take (Lead With This)

Ross Harkness is asking the right question and giving a good answer.
He's just not going deep enough.

His question: "Why can't high-functioning people execute consistently?"
His answer: Identity + Environment + Habits = Self-Mastery.

That's true. And it's incomplete.

Curtis's answer goes underneath it: Because we are *simultaneously justified and sinners*
(simul justus et peccator). We are simultaneously capable and broken. Not one or the other.
Both, at the same time, always. That tension isn't a problem to be fixed with better habits.
It's the engine of formation — if you know how to work with it instead of against it.

Harkness is behavioral. Curtis is theological. Harkness says "change your identity."
Curtis says "your identity is already secured — now let that truth change how you live."

The difference matters enormously for the people Curtis is reaching.
A pastor who can't stop people-pleasing doesn't need an identity shift. He needs to know
that his worth isn't in anyone's approval — it's already settled. Then the habits follow.

---

## Where They Agree

**Identity is upstream of behavior.** Both say this. You cannot consistently do
what you don't believe yourself to be. Ross says manufacture a new identity. Curtis says
receive the one you've already been given. Same diagnosis, different prescription.

**The system must serve the person, not the other way around.**
Ross's "CEO Schedule" and Curtis's operating calendar are the same thing — structured
time that protects high-leverage work. J5 is the operational layer for this.

**You are the bottleneck.** Ross says remove yourself as the bottleneck through delegation
and SOPs. Curtis experiences this daily — he's the pastor, founder, CEO, and father who
is the single point of failure for too many systems. J5 exists precisely to solve this.

**Formation is compound interest.** Both frameworks understand that small, consistent
actions compound over time. Ross calls it habits. Curtis calls it discipleship.
Same principle. Different vocabulary.

---

## Where Curtis Goes Further

**The "why" behind the breakdown.** Harkness says people fail to execute because of
identity misalignment and poor habits. Full stop. Curtis would say: there's also sin,
woundedness, fear, and the deep human need for approval that no habit can fix.
The Four Faces (The Hider, The Striver, The Critic, The Pretender) each represent
a way people cope with this gap. Ross doesn't have language for this. F&F does.

**Rest as theology, not strategy.** Ross talks about the 4-Hour Workday as a productivity
achievement — you can run your business in 4 hours if you're efficient enough.
Curtis's Sabbath is non-negotiable not because it's efficient, but because God commanded it.
Rest is not a reward for high performance. It is a declaration that you are not God.
That's a fundamentally different posture — and it's more durable under pressure.

**The Performance Flywheel has a soul problem.** Ross's system is designed to make
the business run without you — self-improving, data-driven, scalable. That's valuable.
But for a pastor, the danger is optimization without presence. A church that "runs itself"
has lost something essential. The shepherd has to be in the field. J5 handles the system
so Curtis can be present — not so the system can replace his presence.

**Transformation vs. optimization.** Harkness optimizes. Curtis forms.
Optimization asks: how do I perform better? Formation asks: who am I becoming?
Both matter. But for the leaders Curtis serves — burned-out pastors, high-functioning
executives who feel empty despite success — optimization got them into the problem.
Formation is the way out.
